When I first transitioned to remote work, I thought the lack of a commute meant I had “won” at adulthood. I could start early, finish late, and squeeze in chores during calls. But about six months in, I realized I hadn’t actually gained free time—I had just erased the walls between my professional and personal life.

The “always-on” trap doesn’t happen overnight. It’s a slow bleed of boundaries until you realize you’re checking Slack while brushing your teeth. If you’re feeling frayed, look for these seven subtle signs that your remote setup has turned into a 24/7 grind.

1. You use “asynchronous” as an excuse for 10:00 PM emails

Many remote companies pride themselves on asynchronous communication. It sounds efficient, but it often becomes a justification for sending non-urgent requests at all hours. If you find yourself sending emails at 9:00 PM because “it’s not urgent, they can read it tomorrow,” you are training your colleagues to expect late-night availability. You aren’t being helpful; you’re setting a pace that makes you feel like you’re constantly behind.

2. Your “commute” is just a walk to the kitchen

If your morning routine consists of rolling out of bed, grabbing coffee, and opening your laptop within five minutes, you’ve lost your mental transition period. Without a physical commute, you need a psychological one. When you jump straight into the inbox, your brain never enters “work mode”—it just stays in a state of low-level anxiety. If you haven’t changed out of your pajamas or stepped outside before starting, you are blurring the lines before the day even begins.

3. You view household chores as “breaks”

Doing a load of laundry or unloading the dishwasher during a meeting gap is a common remote perk. However, if you are multitasking your domestic life into your workday, you never actually take a break. You aren’t resting; you’re just swapping one type of labor for another. By the time 5:00 PM hits, you’ve worked eight hours and completed three hours of chores, leaving you feeling exhausted without having actually achieved a sense of “downtime.”

4. You treat every notification like a fire alarm

Pay attention to your physical reaction when a Slack notification pings or your phone buzzes. If you feel a spike of adrenaline or a sudden tightening in your chest, your nervous system is stuck in high-alert mode. When you work from home, the “office” is always there. If you don’t physically move your laptop out of sight or turn off work notifications on your phone after hours, you are essentially living in your cubicle.

5. You’ve stopped taking full lunch breaks

In an office, even the busiest person usually gets up to walk to the breakroom or grab a salad. At home, eating a sandwich over your keyboard is the path of least resistance. If you’ve stopped stepping away from your desk to eat, you’ve lost the one guaranteed period of the day where you aren’t producing value for an employer. Skipping lunch isn’t a sign of productivity; it’s a sign that you’ve lost your autonomy.

6. Your “to-do” list never has an ending

When you work in an office, you physically leave the space. That act provides a closing ceremony for the day. At home, the to-do list is always in your line of sight. If your list grows by three items every time you complete one, you are likely suffering from the “remote creep,” where you feel like you should be finishing just one more thing because you’re already home. You need a formal “shutdown ritual” to signal to your brain that the workday is done.

7. You feel guilty for being “away”

Look at your status indicator. If you find yourself manually setting it to “Active” when you step away for a glass of water, or if you feel a pang of anxiety when you don’t respond to a message within five minutes, you are working under the weight of digital surveillance. This is the ultimate sign of the always-on trap. You are performing “work” rather than actually working.

How to course-correct

If you recognized more than two of these signs, don’t try to change your entire life by Monday. Start small by implementing these shifts:

  • The Power Down: Physically turn off your computer and place it in a drawer or a different room at 5:30 PM. Out of sight, out of mind.
  • The Artificial Commute: Take a 10-minute walk before you open your laptop and a 10-minute walk after you close it. This acts as a bookend for your day.
  • The Notification Silence: Use “Focus” modes on your phone to automatically block work apps once your scheduled hours are up.
  • Scheduled “Out of Office”: If you work across time zones, put your working hours in your calendar and stick to them.

Reclaiming your time requires active, intentional effort. The goal isn’t to be a worse employee, but to be a sustainable one. By setting these boundaries now, you prevent the eventual burnout that makes people quit remote work entirely.

Once you’ve identified which of these habits is draining you the most, the next step is to choose one boundary to enforce this week. You might be surprised at how much more focused you are when you actually allow yourself to be “off.”
