When I started working remotely full-time, I treated my Slack notification sound like a digital bell hop. Every ping meant I was “at work.” If I didn’t respond within sixty seconds, I felt like I was failing my team. Two years later, I found myself staring at a blank screen at 4:00 PM, physically present but unable to process a simple email. My brain felt like a browser with 400 tabs open.

If you find yourself constantly checking your phone under the table at dinner or feeling a spike of adrenaline every time your laptop dings, you are likely suffering from Slack fatigue. This isn’t just “being busy”; it’s a specific kind of physiological drain caused by being tethered to a digital tether that never goes silent.

The Physical Red Flags of Over-Connectivity

Before you decide that remote work isn’t for you, look for the physical symptoms. Professional exhaustion doesn’t always show up as a lack of motivation. Often, it shows up as physical tension.

Do you have a persistent headache behind your eyes by midday? Do you notice yourself holding your breath while reading a message thread? These are signs that your nervous system is trapped in a “fight or flight” loop. When we work in an office, we have natural breaks—walking to a meeting, the coffee machine chatter, the visual cue of a coworker leaving their desk. In a digital-only environment, those cues are gone. Your brain assumes that if the green dot is on, you are available. If you aren’t resetting your own boundaries, your brain stays in a state of high-alert, waiting for the next notification.

The “Always-On” Audit: A Practical Exercise

You cannot fix a problem you haven’t measured. For the next three days, perform an audit of your communication habits. Keep a physical notepad next to your keyboard. Every time you switch from a task to check a notification, put a tally mark on the paper.

At the end of the day, look at your tally. If you have 50 marks, you aren’t doing deep work; you are doing “interrupt-driven” work. You aren’t productive; you are reactive.

Once you have your data, look at the nature of the messages. Are they urgent, or are they “FYIs” that could have been an email or a document comment? Most Slack fatigue comes from the medium itself—it encourages low-stakes, high-frequency interruptions that destroy your focus.

Tactics to Reclaim Your Focus

If you want to stay remote but lose the fatigue, you have to change the culture of your own digital workspace. You don’t need permission to be human.

  • The 90-Minute Offline Block: Every morning, put your status to “Focusing - Back at 10:30 AM.” Close the application entirely. If you have a task that requires actual brainpower, do it during this window. If the world is ending, your team has your phone number.
  • Audit Your Notifications: Go into your settings and disable the “Show previews” feature and the sound alerts. Seeing a snippet of a message on your screen is just as distracting as reading the whole thing. If the sound is gone, you control when you check, rather than the app controlling your attention.
  • The “Batching” Method: Instead of responding to messages as they arrive, set three “communication windows” per day: 10:00 AM, 1:00 PM, and 4:30 PM. Tell your immediate team: “I’m moving to a batch communication schedule to finish my project work. I’ll be checking Slack at these times.”

Redefining Your “Remote” Identity

The biggest trap of remote work is the feeling that your presence is measured by your digital activity. I used to think that being “green” on Slack meant I was a good employee. But when I looked at my output, my best work came from the hours when I was not on Slack.

Being a top-tier remote worker isn’t about being the fastest to hit “reply.” It’s about delivering results that make your communication cadence irrelevant. If you are producing high-quality work, your manager will rarely care if you took thirty minutes to reply to a non-urgent question.

If you find that your company culture truly demands second-by-second availability, you have a management problem, not a remote-work problem. In that case, you have to decide if you are willing to trade your mental health for a specific company’s communication preference.

Where to Go From Here

Reducing digital noise is a skill you have to practice every single day. If you don’t build a fence around your time, someone else will walk right through the middle of it. Once you’ve audited your usage and set your boundaries, the next step is to communicate those changes to your stakeholders so they aren’t caught off guard.

If you are ready to shift your workflow, consider how you can start implementing one of the tactics mentioned above starting tomorrow morning.
